    Tokenistic Infrastructure – NZ vs. Japan

    Objective: Evaluate cultural integration in public infrastructure to distinguish performative tokenism from practical equity using the Equity Adaptive Framework (EAF).

    • New Zealand (Case A): Integration of "Marae-inspired" motifs into secular infrastructure (bridges, highways, railway stations) primarily for symbolic inclusion.
    • Japan (Case B): Functional neutrality maintained; sacred symbols reserved for authentic cultural sites, infrastructure prioritizes efficiency and accessibility.

    EAF Principle Alignment: Intent, Harm Prevention, Proportionality, Accountability, Equity, Bias Correction

    Analysis & Findings:

    • Intent: Case A is performative, focusing on optics; Case B reflects practical equity.
    • Harm & Opportunity Cost: Case A diverts resources from high-harm areas (housing, healthcare); Case B avoids alienation and preserves cultural integrity.
    • Proportionality: NZ's symbolic infrastructure is disproportionate to systemic needs; Japan’s approach maintains balance between function and cultural expression.
    • Contextual Integrity of Sacred Symbols: NZ “Marae-like frames” dilute meaning; Torii gates in Japan reserved for authentic cultural spaces.
    • Fail-Safe Trigger – Bad Faith & Conflation: Triggered in NZ due to performative claims of “cultural safety” without substantive equity; not triggered in Japan.

    Outcome & Prescription: Redirect NZ infrastructure funding from decorative motifs to Mandatory Restitution in housing, healthcare, and social services. Future projects must pass the EAF Contextual Integrity Test, ensuring authentic cultural intent.

    Status & Impact: Case study documented for EAF database; serves as a reference for ethical evaluation of symbolic vs. functional interventions.
