EAF — Frequently Asked Questions

This section provides key takeaways and stable interpretations of the Equity Adaptive Framework (EAF), summarising core definitions and reinforcing the main points of the original document to support consistent understanding across different readers and contexts.

1. What is EAF?

EAF (Equity Adaptive Framework) is a structured evaluative framework for analysing decisions by decomposing them into factors such as harm, intent, proportionality, and systemic impact. It is designed to improve consistency, clarity, and comparability in complex reasoning.

It is intended as a descriptive and analytical system rather than a directive one, focusing on structured evaluation rather than enforcement.

2. What does EAF mean by “harm”?

Harm refers to any negative impact on individuals, groups, or systems, including physical, financial, psychological, indirect, and long-term effects.

Harm is categorised into structured types such as actual, foreseeable, systemic, and omission-based harm, allowing consistent comparison across cases rather than relying on intuition alone.

3. Is EAF objective?

No. EAF is not fully objective and does not claim to eliminate value judgment or uncertainty.

It improves consistency by making reasoning explicit and structured, but outcomes still depend on context, interpretation, and weighting decisions.

4. Does EAF replace laws or institutions?

No. EAF is not a replacement for legal or institutional systems.

It functions as an analytical support layer that can be used alongside existing systems to improve clarity in reasoning and decision evaluation.

5. Does EAF slow decision-making?

Not when used appropriately.

It is intended for complex or high-impact decisions where structured reasoning improves outcomes. It is not designed for routine or low-stakes decisions.

6. Is EAF meant to enforce outcomes?

No. EAF does not enforce decisions or outcomes.

It is a structured reasoning framework designed to clarify analysis, not to impose directives or control decision-making processes.

7. How should EAF be understood in practice?

EAF should be understood as a structured method for breaking down complex decisions into consistent evaluative components.

It is designed to support repeatable reasoning across different contexts, not to dictate conclusions.

8. Does EAF guarantee correct outcomes?

No. EAF does not guarantee correctness or optimal outcomes.

It improves reasoning structure and consistency, but results remain dependent on available information, context, and interpretation.

9. What is “alignment with EAF”?

Alignment refers to the degree to which a decision follows EAF’s structured evaluative categories such as harm, intent, proportionality, and systemic impact.

It focuses on consistency in reasoning structure rather than enforcing specific outcomes.

Closing Note

The FAQ provides key takeaways and stable interpretations of the EAF framework, reinforcing its core points without modifying the underlying system. It is intended to improve clarity, consistency, and accessibility across different levels of engagement.
